# Second Blog Post

We were able to finalise features that made our blog have unique value. The challenge we always stumbled across was, “how do we create participation and communication between people in the badminton community without replicating current social groups on social media”, “how will this be different?”, “what can we address that social media doesnt” Some ideas we liked included:

### map for court information/ leveraging quick information that requires less time to search specific courts
- RSVP system: register without contacting an admin (reduce stress with signing up)
- provide information of other users (less hesitation to join a social knowing others skill levels)
- Forum system for gear questions, FAQ and knowledge about courts in one hub for Australian based users.

**Within the rsvp system we found this to be a great way to reduce the intimation barrier:** 
Current social media applications arent designed with an rsvp function, which requires the instagram handler to manage message and coordinate people. This asks for people who are interested, to message someone they may not know directly. Creating barriers such as:

* Exposing for users who may not want to expose their personal social media profile
* This also requires for users to admit their skill level, availability or even approval before they can join. 
* Fear of rejection


To solve this, we want users to feel they can improve their skill levels without the anxiety induced interaction. It eliminates admin gatekeeping where users can apply to sessions directly like a booking system, whilst details are important for users to input, it doesn't require them to feel judged.  Users can register for sessions directly, with their self-reported skill levels visible to others in the group. Not only does this maintain transparency with who's going, but you can familiarise yourself with others before the session. 

### Trade-off considerations
However, this decision introduces trade-offs. There could be potential skill mismatches, such as advanced players playing in beginner comps, could this ruin the playing experience for others? We find ourselves to rely on trust-based self-reporting being visible to other profiles, whilst mismatch can always happen. Honesty for other users to view will help see who is registered and regulate a better selection of appropriate sessions. We're explicitly not building features that enforce users to be perfectly skilled in sessions they have no interest in. Taking away any ratings that could shame users, no algorithmic sorting. This keeps our scope manageable while aligning with our core group goal of reducing intimidation for the community. 
These decisions will shape our technical requirements, where the system may need a database structure that links users, sessions, and skill levels. The profile visibility means our system must show the appropriate information stores about users e.g their number of attended events, skill level and bio of each profile. 


Our group established our goals for the website with clear artifacts such as sitemaps to help us refer back to. We plan to finalise our design system by finalising fonts, cta buttons and colour scheme for our community. This will ensure the website will be consistent throughout when coding individually. We also came to finalising our wireframes this week, we initially designed them on mobile for our core design but soon realised organising events might be more preferable for users on desktop.
